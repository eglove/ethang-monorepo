Why I Don’t Allow Comments: Reclaiming Focus in a Noisy World

Prioritizing thoughtful engagement over instant reactions

In our increasingly interconnected world, there's a pervasive expectation to comment, react, and engage instantly. Whether it’s a LinkedIn post or a personal blog, the absence of a comment section often raises questions. However, my decision to disable comments isn’t a dismissal of dialogue. Instead, it’s a deliberate choice to cultivate a different kind of engagement—one that values depth, reflection, and a healthier digital environment for everyone.

The Algorithmic Influence on Our Minds

Social media algorithms are incredibly sophisticated, designed with one primary goal: to maximize "engagement." They achieve this by feeding us content that elicits strong, often immediate, reactions. This constant, rapid stream of information has profoundly impacted our collective attention span. Research by Gloria Mark, a professor of informatics at the University of California, Irvine, highlights a significant decline in average attention spans on screens—from 2.5 minutes in 2004 to a mere 47 seconds in recent years. This incessant "attention switching" can lead to increased stress and errors. Short-form video content and a barrage of notifications further train our brains to crave quick bursts of information, making sustained focus an increasingly challenging endeavor.

Beyond simply shortening our attention, these algorithms frequently push us towards content designed to spark disagreement. They learn what keeps us scrolling, and unfortunately, controversy and outrage are highly effective engagement drivers. Studies have shown that social media algorithms amplify emotionally charged and divisive content, even when users report that such content makes them feel worse about political outgroups. This dynamic often creates echo chambers and filter bubbles, where we are primarily exposed to viewpoints that confirm our existing biases, making genuine, nuanced discussion incredibly difficult. The underlying goal isn’t productive debate, but rather to keep us hooked, often by stoking conflict and polarization.

My Personal Path to Disconnecting

To illustrate this, consider my own experience: LinkedIn is, in fact, the only "social media" platform I still actively use. Yet even there, the algorithmic pull towards engagement can be insidious. I once accumulated around 1,800 connections, most of whom I didn’t know and had no idea why we were connected in the first place. This stemmed from a time when I mistakenly believed that a large network signified something meaningful. I’ve since undertaken a massive purge, removing every connection I possibly could. Due to platform bugs or perhaps deleted accounts, I couldn’t remove every single one, but when I visit "My Network," it’s empty. LinkedIn now serves as a simple professional profile and a platform for my newsletter—nothing more. Despite this intentional detachment, I still find myself occasionally drawn into ridiculous comment threads when LinkedIn’s algorithm recommends posts that disagree with my own content. This constant exposure to engineered conflict is not healthy, and I am actively doing my best to opt out.

My approach to consuming information is also a direct counter to the algorithmic curation prevalent today. I do not rely on curated news sites or algorithmically driven content feeds. Instead, I leverage the power of RSS feeds. I subscribe to literally hundreds of RSS feeds, including those from YouTube channels, ensuring that I receive content directly from its source, sorted by "last published" rather than by "most controversial" or "most engaging." This method provides a much better appreciation for personal and professional blogs, allowing me to discover and follow voices without the overwhelming noise of traditional news sites. I find just looking at the homepages of excessively noisy news sites like InfoWorld or GeekWire to be anxiety-inducing. This is, in my opinion, a better way to engage with the web: to depend on interlinked sites and your own curated sources for new information, fostering a more intentional and less manipulated consumption of content.

The Erosion of Listening and the Entitlement to Speak

When every post is an invitation to comment, we risk losing the fundamental art of simply listening or reading. There's a growing sense of entitlement to have one’s immediate thoughts heard, regardless of whether they add value or are well-considered. This instant-reaction culture often bypasses critical thinking, favoring emotional responses over reasoned arguments. When comments are disabled, or individuals are blocked for persistent argumentative behavior, it can be perceived as an affront—a denial of their "right" to express themselves. However, the purpose of a blog or a professional post is often to share insights, not to open a free-for-all forum for every fleeting thought or manufactured disagreement.

Reclaiming Space for Thought and Real Life

By removing the comment section, I aim to create a space for focused consumption. It encourages readers to absorb the content, reflect on it, and form their own conclusions without the immediate pressure to respond or be drawn into tangential arguments. If you disagree with something I’ve written, that’s perfectly fine. You don’t have to let me know. I’m not isolating myself by not hearing you out in particular; nor is it a refusal to hear "differing viewpoints." We are strangers, and we don’t HAVE to be in conversation. This approach prioritizes the quality of the shared content and the mental well-being of both the creator and the audience, fostering an environment where information can be considered without the constant noise and pressure of the algorithmic feedback loop.

Ultimately, I believe people should seriously consider stepping away from social media. Stop thinking you need to be "heard" by the entire world. Stop believing you need to be "part of the conversation" with complete strangers who are being actively manipulated by social media algorithms. Instead, read, write, and reflect. Focus on the real world without expecting that everything you say should be a response to or a reaction to someone else. Go out and simply be yourself.

No comments here. Go outside and talk to a real person!
