In today’s digital world, information drives decisions. Software engineers are central to this process. While often perceived as "problem solvers" or "innovators," a closer look reveals a simpler truth: many of us function much like data brokers. Our core task is to ensure the right information reaches the right people, efficiently, enabling better decisions.

The Unseen Pipelines: Our Brokerage Business

What is the fundamental role of a software engineer? We build systems. These systems are essentially sophisticated tools for moving, transforming, and presenting data. From creating APIs' that deliver specific data points to designing ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) pipelines that process vast amounts of raw data into usable formats, our work is centered on managing information flow.

We connect various data sources, clean and validate datasets, maintain data integrity, and present insights through dashboards, applications, or reports. We are the architects of the digital infrastructure, ensuring that critical business data reaches those who need it. We typically do not generate the data, nor do we make the final decisions based on it. Our role is to facilitate. We are, in essence, the middlemen.

The Automation Mindset: Facilitating vs. Solving

A common perspective within software engineering is that automating a process means the problem is "solved." We develop a new service, optimize a database, or deploy a feature, often considering the task complete. This work is indeed valuable.

The real value often lies not in building the machine itself, but in ensuring the right fuel gets to the right engine at the right time.

However, this view often misses the larger picture. Did we truly solve the business challenge, or did we create a more efficient information delivery mechanism? More often, it is the latter. The actual business problem—whether it’s declining sales, operational inefficiencies, or customer retention issues—is ultimately addressed by those who make decisions based on the information we provide. We enable, we facilitate, we broker the insights that lead to solutions.

Behind the Screens: The Data Undercurrent

Forget the headlines about cutting-edge algorithms; the daily grind of a software engineer often involves far less glamorous, yet equally critical, tasks. We are the ones plumbing the depths of databases, extracting raw data, and pushing it through intricate networks to its destination. This isn’t about grand innovation; it’s about the relentless, often invisible, movement of information.

The Reservoirs and Conduits

Our work frequently involves building and maintaining the vast digital reservoirs—the data warehouses and data lakes—where information is stored. But storage is only half the battle. The other half is constructing the conduits: the automated scripts, the reporting tools, the dashboard generators that pull specific, filtered data from these reservoirs and deliver it to the right desks. It’s a constant process of collection, transformation, and distribution, much like an intricate, underground water system.

The Invisible Flow of Business

Every interaction in a modern business, from a customer placing an order to an employee clocking in, generates data. Our role is to capture this data, process it, and ensure it flows to where it can be analyzed. We build the hidden pathways that move transactional records, user activity logs, and system performance metrics. The end-user rarely sees this complex ballet of bytes, only the polished interface or the final report. We are the silent operators, ensuring the currents of information never cease, always flowing to the next point of need.

This continuous movement of data, often behind the scenes and without direct interaction with the end-decision, solidifies our position. We are the essential, yet non-decision-making, intermediaries.

The Interface: Where Data Meets Decision

The flow of data, from its raw form to actionable insight is a continuous chain. Software engineers are responsible for every link, including the crucial interface where data becomes truly usable. We design and build the digital storefronts and control panels that allow people to interact with, search, filter, and visualize the information we’ve meticulously moved and transformed.

Consider the simple act of searching for a product, applying a filter to a report, or visualizing sales trends on a dashboard. These seemingly effortless interactions are the result of complex engineering. Our role here is to ensure that users can intuitively navigate, query, and manipulate information. Making these complex interactions happen at the speed of thought—meaning, without friction or delay, allowing users to instantly access and act upon the insights they need.

It’s about translating the vast, intricate data landscape into a digestible, actionable experience. Without this interactive layer, the perfectly brokered data would remain locked away, inaccessible to those who need it most for their everyday operations and decisions. Software engineers are critical middlemen, making the data useful and immediate, from its deepest storage to its final presentation.

The Inflated Reality: Perception, Pay, and the Job Market

A significant challenge arises from the perception that software engineers are more than just sophisticated data brokers. There's a prevailing narrative, often fueled by media and industry hype, that casts us as the "true innovators" and indispensable heroes of the digital age. This inflated view can lead to a certain arrogance, an assumption that our role extends far beyond the administrative and facilitative work of ensuring data flow. We are increasingly expected to act as "product owners," possessing deep business acumen, market understanding, and strategic foresight—all while maintaining the complex technical infrastructure.

This overestimation of the software engineer’s direct economic control has a tangible consequence: inflated compensation. While our technical skills are specialized and demand expertise, the core function of brokering data, from backend pipelines to user interfaces is fundamentally an administrative task, albeit one of high complexity. Yet salaries often soar far beyond what might be considered equitable when compared to other demanding, skilled trades that form the backbone of our physical economy—truck driving, welding, electrical work. These professions require years of training, carry significant physical risks, and are absolutely essential for daily life, yet they rarely command the same financial rewards.

This disparity fuels a peculiar, yet accepted, industry practice: frequent job changes. Many software engineers cycle through companies every one to two years, primarily driven by the pursuit of ever-higher compensation. This isn’t necessarily about building a sustainable career or creating long-term value within a single organization; it’s about playing an unethical job-hop market game. The perception of being an irreplaceable "innovator" allows individuals to leverage this demand for short-term financial gains, rather than having genuine "skin in the game" by committing to a business’s long-term success.

If the aspiration is to become a millionaire, the path should ideally involve genuine value creation through entrepreneurship—building a business, taking on significant risk, and solving market problems directly. Relying on a market dynamic that overpays for a facilitative role, enabling a constant stream of job changes for more cash, is a less sustainable and arguably less impactful route to wealth. The job of a data broker, while vital, should be compensated fairly for its administrative and facilitative nature. With pay more equivalent to that of skilled trades, rather than serving as a shortcut to extreme wealth or a justification for a culture of transient loyalty.

Beyond the Hype: The Everyday Reality

Media portrayals often cast software engineers as the powerful architects of the digital age, the brilliant minds single-handedly shaping economies and controlling society’s levers. This narrative, while flattering, misses the mark. The reality for most software engineers is far less glamorous and far more grounded. We are not the heroes dictating market trends or the puppet masters pulling the strings of global finance.

Instead, we are the diligent, often unseen, workers ensuring that the digital gears turn smoothly. Our daily work involves the meticulous task of making information accessible and usable. This applies not just to business operations, but to the consumer products and services that underpin modern life: online shopping platforms, digital documents, GPS navigation, social media feeds, streaming entertainment, and online investing tools. In every one of these domains, it’s about building the systems that allow people to interact with information effectively, make informed choices, and navigate their daily lives with greater ease.

Our contribution is significant, but it’s one of enablement and support, not control. We are the administrative backbone, the quiet facilitators who ensure the digital world functions, allowing others to be the decision-makers and the drivers of their respective fields. The economy isn’t controlled by our lines of code; it’s powered by the information our systems broker, enabling countless individuals to do their jobs and live their lives.

Embracing the Middleman: A Path to Effective Contribution

Perhaps it is time to acknowledge our primary role: that of sophisticated data brokers. This perspective does not diminish our contribution; it clarifies its importance. By focusing on:

  • Clarity of Information: Ensuring data is not just available, but also understandable and actionable.

  • Speed of Delivery: Optimizing systems for timely insights.

  • Reliability: Building stable systems that guarantee data integrity and availability.

  • Security: Protecting the sensitive information we manage.

We empower organizations to navigate complexity, make informed decisions, and succeed. Our value is not in making the final call, but in providing the clear insights necessary for those calls to be made effectively. In a data-rich world, the software engineer, as a data broker, remains a vital component.

To foster a more sustainable and equitable industry, companies should shift their focus from overpaying so-called "rockstar engineers" who often jump ship after a couple of years for the next big offer. Instead, the emphasis should be on building more stable business models where the individual worker’s compensation is less directly tied to the company’s entire business success. This likely entails hiring more people for a fairer, more balanced wage, and actively challenging the wild expectations many software engineers have developed—the notion that passing a tech interview somehow entitles them to live as successful entrepreneurs. True entrepreneurial success, and the millionaire status that might accompany it, should come from taking genuine risks, building one’s own business, and creating direct market value, not from playing an inflated job market game.

Built with an appreciation for the middlemen.
