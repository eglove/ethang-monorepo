Where’d the Team Go?

This will be my second installment of long rants that guarantees I will never find a job again. But it really comes down to is personal reflections on what I am seeing in an industry compared to the good and bad times I’ve had working in it.

Here is an AI analysis of this blog:

The blog post, "Where’d the Team Go?," is a candid critique of the modern software development industry’s cultural shortcomings. The author argues that a widespread fear of clear communication and accountability is leading to dysfunctional teams and low-quality software. They contrast this with established methodologies like Acceptance Test-Driven Development (ATDD), which they believe foster clarity and prevent bugs from the outset. A key point of contention is the use of vague language and a "what do you mean?" defensive reflex, which the author sees has a symptom of a broader issue with anxiety and burnout. The post also expresses skepticism about the current obsession with AI, arguing that it simply magnifies existing problems when not built upon a foundation of human-driven, clear requirements. Ultimately, the author suggests that the industry’s focus on "fast-paced" environments over genuine teamwork and quality processes is detrimental, contrasting this with their perception of more effective, disciplined approaches found in military or highly successful tech companies.

And now for my part:

The obsession with AI/LLM persists even though objectively speaking, I have seen no great revolution. Google is making more money as they always do. Start-ups are burning with hot fire and crashing as they always do. Companies and consumers with no relation to tech are sick and f***ing tired of hearing about our stupid trends. As they always have.

But what I want to write about today on my porch is testing. Or quality control. Or something like that. Let’s just jump into it.

I’ve been reading the SWEBOK (Software Engineering Body of Knowledge) Guide, and just in the first chapter it discusses BDD vs. ATDD for methods of writing and handling requirements. BDD is preferred by non-technical stakeholders because it means requirements are written in plain English. ATDD, on the other hand, is more precise. It means agreeing on technical test cases, writing automated tests, and only when a test fails a software engineer may modify production code. This is beneficial. It means clear agreement on requirements, translating those to automated tests, and passing said tests.

I can’t tell you how much wasted time in meetings this saves. People complain about too many meetings, but that’s not the problem. The problem is there aren’t enough useful meetings with actual communication. The typical flow seems to be a project manager and CTO meet with a client, they write up a very loose BDD document in Microsoft Word, then create a backlog of tickets. These tickets are rarely written with any detail. Often half-sentence notes with an imaginary reference to a Word doc.

Software engineers are left guessing, asking questions, and honestly treated like morons by the one who poorly wrote tickets to begin with. So they pull teeth and guess. Who cares, they’ll just quit in 2 years anyway. Or find a way to get “promoted” so they too can spend all their time in Confluence.

Meetings, from this point, become pointless standups where you either say, “no update, no blockers” and get a smile. Or say, “I don’t understand what this 3-word ticket is trying to say,” and get a sigh. Welcome to the doghouse. Start applying now.

But what if we followed the standards of the SWEBOK Guide? It means agreeing on test cases. And the feature is complete when these test cases pass. Software engineers make no changes until they have test cases.

Excuse me, what? It can be this simple? Why have I never worked in an environment like this? The closest equivalent was a requirement to keep test coverage >80%. The result of that was 0 bugs. Conflicts were known immediately before pushing any code.

“What was that? Zero bugs? That’s not possible.” I hear you say. But it is. Deterministic computer programs are infallible. It is only humans who make mistakes.

I fear. And this is where I really get into trouble. If you are a recruiter or hiring, please stop reading now. I fear there is a culture in software that doesn’t allow for correcting mistakes. You can make mistakes, sure, but god forbid you ever admit to it, or anyone suggests it be fixed.

This is not the same as recognizing mistakes. Shipping software with 100 bugs is considered acceptable. “We’ll just iterate.” “Put that in a bug ticket.” But if anyone suggests agreeing on requirements, API contracts, test cases, talking about ways to avoid mistakes to begin with… welcome to the doghouse. Start applying now.

This is without a doubt cultural sensitivity. If your ESLint configuration gives you an error, “no useless assignment,” and you tell your peer that their code has a useless assignment, even just quoting the error, expect a call from your manager. 100% of the time, on every team. I have never seen an exception to this. I have never gone to a manager and said, “He called me useless. That language is inappropriate!” But I imagine that’s what must be happening. “We don’t use language like that here.” You’re in software, yes, you do. Imagine being offended by a very accurate and useful ESLint rule and blaming it on your peer. I can’t. But I’ve seen it, a lot.

Slowly, people have just stopped communicating altogether. You put on a mask, learn to say the right words, keep your head down, and get really good at redirecting disagreement or problems in a way that puts everyone else on the defensive and makes you look ignorant.

Let’s say person B says, “Our bug backlog is very long and growing. I think we can tighten up static analysis and improve test coverage, I’ve written up a detailed plan on how this can be done with no real additional time cost.” Person A responds with, “What do you mean?”

I find the question, “What do you mean?” in contexts like this to be infuriating. I have only ever seen defensive and sensitive people act like this. When I say, “defensive and sensitive,” I don’t mean in the realm of healthy. I mean in the realm of their teammates should be finding ways to make them aware and get them help. And I do believe there is an industry-wide psychology diagnosis to be made that affects everyone.

Burnout is caused by getting stuck in fight or flight and frying your nervous system. There is a deep anxiety and fear hidden under the question. Which is naturally designed to redirect the other person to be on the defensive. It is not a question asked in earnest. It’s a defensive reflex.

“What do I mean?” Person B could say, “I’ve made it very clear, I have written out a plan that you haven’t looked at. There are 1,000 user reported bugs on the backlog. What do I mean??”

There’s no good response to this. Google Gemini suggests turning it back on them. Something along the lines of, “I think I was very clear, what about what I just said is confusing to you?”

Oof. Imagine being in this standup. You probably have. This isn’t going well. Person B, welcome to the doghouse. Start applying now.

What’s so fascinating to me is whether I’m in this situation or witnessing it, I have always seen Person A as the toxic aggressor. Even if I’m the one asking, “What do you mean?” and someone calls me out on it, I will apologize and recognize the need to listen, think critically, read, and get back to them with a real question or opinion.

But if I’m the person to say, “What part is unclear?” and I’m accused of being rude, we are not friends. And I’m livid anytime I see this done to someone else.

In a culture of sensitivity and damaged nervous systems, Person A is seen as “just asking a question.” In a healthy social culture someone would probably break the tension by making fun of Person A. “What do YOU mean? What do you mean by ‘what’? That depends on what the definition of ‘is’ is!” A healthy team would move on and consider the solution at hand. Giving Person A the space to breathe and recover from a stupid question.

Not in tech. Instead, everyone sits in silence, watching two people play stupid games, until one quits or gets fired.

And so we seem to have a fear of communication and clarity. Developers guessing at requirements, broken product is OK, and now people are using AI to generate documentation and tests. Proclaiming how efficient they are because of how many tokens they paid for. Counting them like poker chips, forgetting that only the house wins.

There is a clear proven standard outlined by ATDD in the SWEBOK Guide. A clear path between requirements, tests, and code. This is communication. Talking about what’s wanted, documenting conversation, agreeing on test cases, and building the product.

Where is the room for AI generation here? If you AI generate your documentation with clients, there is a 100% chance of misinterpretation. Not 50%, 100%. If developers just start slapping keyboards based on a verbal idea or Figma designs, there is a 100% chance of misinterpretation. If they don’t write tests and just AI generate them after, creating random inputs into random functions, there is no possible way code is being written to requirements. Because no technical requirements have been written and there is no definition of done.

“We’ll just iterate.” On what? You have nothing. No standard. No agreement. No goals. Just loose words and random ideas that everyone has a different interpretation of. Including your AI.

“We’re not Google.” That’s clear. But last I checked communication was free and saves time. So I don’t know what the argument is here.

All of this sounds to me like, “I’m afraid. If I communicate, I will offend.” Yes, that’s the risk. That is damn near the definition of communication.

I don’t put a lot of weight on my military experience. It wasn’t much. And I have been yelled at and screamed at by recruiters for putting it on my résumé. For most of my tech career it felt safer to hide. Because while it helps for most jobs, in tech you’re assumed to not be a culture fit. Because military culture isn’t sensitivity culture. You get chewed out or made fun of for, “What do you mean?” Everyone knows it’s a jackass thing to do. Whether it’s on purpose or an anxiety-induced reflex. It’s a habit that should be worked on.

But when I think about what a “team” is, or what “leadership” is, or what “clarity” means, I often think about the military. Where if you play games, people lose their lives. You don’t get the luxury of a shit turnover rate and never having to learn to get along with people.

If you’re Person A, the “what do you mean?” type, you can keep your head down and be one of the three people who stay at the company. While every other asshole is a rotating door every 1–2 years. If you’re one of the assholes, you only have to act polite for 1–2 years before you burnout and quit for another new exciting job. If you’re in the Army, you get to sleep next to each other until you learn to stop acting like children.

My point though, in bringing up the military, is I can’t find teams. I’ve done a lot of interviews, a lot of contracts. If I bring up the concept of “team” as something I’m looking for, I’m sure as hell not getting a call back. No, I need to say, “I’m looking for a FAST-PACED, DYNAMIC, CROSS-FUNCTIONAL ENVIRONMENT WITH FREE MOUNTAIN DEW!!! AND AI!!! I LOVE AI!!!!!!”

I’m not kidding when I say this is the kind of script that gets you hired. Minus the part about Mountain Dew. I believe it’s because people have been permanently operating in a fight or flight mode for a very long time and expect it as normal behavior. If you’re not absolutely wired for pushing slop as quickly as possible, you’re not welcome.

That doesn’t mean if you’re not the “fastest,” you’re not welcome, only if you don’t act like it. The “fastest” people are using TDD and communication. Those Google’s of the world that didn’t just beat you to market, but put your broken slop to shame. If you get lucky, maybe they’ll buy you out.

And it’s not just because they make more money and have more engineers. Their product teams are often 7ish people with similar budgets. What makes them more effective is standard processes and communication. The stuff average teams are afraid of; unit tests, integration tests, and E2E tests. Google has this idea of the “three amigos,” product managers, developers, and testers. Sounds familiar, right?

“Unit tests are useless!!” Get out of here. I’m not entertaining this voice of incompetence and fear anymore. It’s time to wrap this up.

The lack of teamwork and communication in this industry has driven me mad. And AI is only multiplying its effects. I don’t think we’ll ever live in a world where everyone we know doesn’t have a laundry list of complaints about the software and computers they depend on. And many people will lean on this, “If it will always be broken, let’s just ship broken.” It will become their mantra, they’ll say it’s why they move so “fast,” but never quantify that with real long-term numbers. This makes it very difficult to be truly happy about ones work.

If anyone claims to be “happy” or “competent” about shipping 100 bugs in two weeks, I fear they don’t know what either of those things feels like. I have felt these things, but they are rare in tech. And I hope everyone gets a moment to reflect and find both.
